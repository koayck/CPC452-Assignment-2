\section{Abstract}

Toxland VR: Hop \& Help is an educational Virtual Reality application that teaches young children to recognise and safely handle toxic or hazardous household materials such as medicines, household poisons, pesticides, and aerosol sprays. Inspired by the classic Snakes and Ladders board game, players roll a virtual dice to move across an interactive board and answer \emph{Betul/Salah} (True/False) questions about safe household practices: correct answers earn points and a short explanation, wrong answers deduct points, while ladder and trap squares on the board provide the familiar advance/fall-back movement. The educational content is presented in Bahasa Melayu. This project converts and adapts an existing Unity3D VR build developed with the Meta SDK for the Meta Quest headset into an XR-compatible build that runs on the PICO 4 headset using the PICO XR SDK. The conversion covers Unity XR settings, controller input mapping, camera rig and locomotion, UI interaction, and Android build configuration. This report documents the VR concept, background study, conversion methodology, system development, and testing evidence confirming that the application runs successfully on the target platform.

\section{VR Concept}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

This section presents the conceptual design of Toxland VR: Hop \& Help, including its theme, intended audience, world design, storyline, storyboard, and team structure.

\subsection{Concept Theme}

The concept theme of Toxland VR is household hazard safety education, delivered through a board-game-inspired progression system. The application is a \textbf{hybrid of an educational and entertainment-focused experience}: it borrows the familiar mechanics of Snakes and Ladders --- ladders that let players climb ahead and traps that send them back --- and pairs each board space with a \emph{Betul/Salah} (True/False) safety question that rewards correct knowledge and corrects misconceptions, reinforcing real-world household safety habits around medicines, household poisons, pesticides, aerosol sprays, and supplements. The educational intent is primary (the learning outcome is the goal), while the game framing, scoring, and rewards exist to sustain motivation and engagement. The design objectives are to help children (i)~recognise potentially hazardous household items, (ii)~adopt safe storage practices, (iii)~understand warning labels and safety symbols, (iv)~behave responsibly around household chemicals, and (v)~retain these lessons through interactive, positively reinforced repetition.

\subsection{Target Audience}

The primary target audience is \textbf{young children, approximately 6--11 years old (lower- to upper-primary school)}. This range is chosen for three reasons. First, it is the developmental window in which children begin to access kitchens, bathrooms, and storage areas with reduced adult supervision, making household-chemical awareness directly relevant to their daily lives. Second, children of this age respond strongly to game-based reward/penalty structures, which the Snakes-and-Ladders mechanic exploits directly. Third, because reading ability varies widely across this band, the interface deliberately pairs short Bahasa Melayu text with icons, colour coding, and audio cues so that the safety content remains accessible to weaker readers. The VR medium is selected because immersive, first-person decision-making is expected to reinforce safety habits more effectively than passive instruction such as posters or textbook lessons, while the playful framing keeps the experience age-appropriate rather than frightening.

\subsection{World Design}

Players enter the colourful world of Toxland, where everyday household hazards are turned into a board-game challenge. The level is a single numbered board laid out as a winding path of tiles in the Snakes-and-Ladders tradition; ladder squares let the player climb ahead and trap squares send them back. Rather than the board being physically divided into rooms, the safety questions placed on its tiles cover the hazards found throughout a typical home:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Medicines} --- reading labels (e.g.\ red labels marking external-use medicines), safe dosage, and storage out of children's reach.
  \item \textbf{Household poisons} --- rat poison and insecticides, and where they may (or may not) be kept.
  \item \textbf{Pesticides and aerosols} --- safe storage away from heat and open flame.
  \item \textbf{Supplements} --- vitamins and iron, and the danger of taking too much at once.
  \item \textbf{Poisoning response} --- what should (and should not) be done if poisoning occurs.
\end{itemize}

Each interactive tile presents a \emph{Betul/Salah} (True/False) question with immediate Right/Wrong feedback and a short explanation. The visual style is bright and cartoon-like to remain non-threatening for young children. The board layout is shown in the level design plan in Section~\ref{sec:level-design}.

\subsection{Storyline}

Players travel across the Toxland board to reach the final space. On each turn they:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Roll a virtual dice and move along the board to the next space.
  \item Land on a space, which presents a household-safety statement.
  \item Decide whether the statement is \emph{Betul} (true) or \emph{Salah} (false) using the VR controller.
  \item Receive immediate Right/Wrong feedback with a short explanation, and gain ($+2$) or lose ($-2$) points accordingly.
  \item Climb a ladder square to advance, or slide back on a trap square, in the usual Snakes-and-Ladders fashion.
\end{enumerate}

The game ends when the player reaches the final space, at which point the final score is tallied and displayed on the end screen.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

\subsection{Existing Applications}

To position Toxland VR against the state of the art, we reviewed three existing, peer-reviewed serious-game and VR applications for child safety and hazard education. Table~\ref{tab:background-study} compares them on purpose, platform, and interaction model.

\hline
\textbf{Application} & \textbf{Purpose} & \textbf{Platform} & \textbf{Interaction Model} \\
\hline
Safe City~\cite{safecity2020} & Mobile serious game teaching home, road, and sports safety knowledge and behaviour to primary-school children (grades 4--6) & Mobile phone (Android/iOS) & Avatar-based role-play; touch-screen taps through scenario mini-games \\

\hline
\end{tabularx}

\textbf{Educational objectives.} All three reviewed systems target child safety, but each addresses a different hazard domain: Safe City covers general home/road/sports safety~\cite{safecity2020}, FSCHOOL focuses on fire preparedness~\cite{fsfire2022}, and Khan~et~al.\ address road safety~\cite{roadsafety2021}. None specifically target \emph{household toxic and chemical materials}, which is the niche Toxland VR fills.

\textbf{Interaction methods.} Safe City is the least immersive --- it is a flat, touch-driven mobile game~\cite{safecity2020}. The fire-preparedness game uses a CAVE projection environment that surrounds the learner but is fixed to specialised hardware~\cite{fsfire2022}. The road-safety system adds embodied, gesture-based input via Kinect and, notably, \emph{adaptive difficulty}~\cite{roadsafety2021}. Toxland VR uses a consumer head-mounted display (PICO 4) with six-degrees-of-freedom controllers, giving room-scale immersion at a far lower cost and footprint than a CAVE.

\textbf{User engagement and learning effectiveness.} A common thread across these works is that game framing --- avatars, missions, points, and reward/penalty feedback --- sustains children's engagement and supports knowledge retention better than passive instruction~\cite{safecity2020,roadsafety2021}. Toxland VR operationalises exactly this principle: each question tile delivers immediate Right/Wrong feedback paired with a short explanation, so the safety lesson is coupled directly to the player's own answer.

\subsection{Key Innovation}

Toxland VR builds on the reviewed applications in three distinct ways:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A board-game progression mechanic.} Rather than free-roaming scenarios, Toxland VR structures learning as a Snakes-and-Ladders journey. This gives young children a clear goal, bite-sized True/False questions, and an intrinsically motivating ``advance vs.\ fall back'' loop that reinforces correct safety knowledge.
  \item \textbf{An under-served hazard domain.} The reviewed systems cover road, fire, and general safety; Toxland VR specifically targets \emph{toxic household materials} (medicines, household poisons, pesticides, aerosols, and supplements) --- a leading cause of childhood poisoning that the surveyed literature does not address.
  \item \textbf{Cross-headset portability.} The same gameplay and educational content, originally built against the Meta SDK for Meta Quest, is re-engineered to run on the PICO XR SDK for the PICO 4 headset. This demonstrates that the educational design is not locked to a single vendor's hardware ecosystem --- the core contribution and the main technical effort of this assignment.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Game Flow}

The game flow (Figure~\ref{fig:gameflow}) is a single loop bounded by the welcome and end screens. On each turn the player rolls the dice and moves along the board to the next space; ladder and trap squares additionally move the player forward or back in the usual Snakes-and-Ladders manner. The score is updated and the loop repeats until the player reaches the final space, where the end screen displays the final score.

\subsection{Interactive Zone \& Triggers}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Dice trigger} --- rolls the dice and produces the movement value (\texttt{DiceManager.totalValue}); the player then advances along the board.
  \item \textbf{Ladder square trigger} --- detects landing on a ladder tile and advances the player, granting a bonus move via \texttt{BtnOKManager}'s \texttt{ExtraMoveEvent}.
  \item \textbf{Trap square trigger} --- detects landing on a trap tile and moves the player back.
  \item \textbf{Question tile trigger} --- presents a \emph{Betul/Salah} (True/False) household-safety statement with Right/Wrong feedback and an explanation panel.
  \item \textbf{Score trigger} --- updates \texttt{ScoreManager} on correct answers ($+2$) and wrong answers ($-2$), and on game completion.
\end{itemize}

\section{System Development}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

This section describes the implementation and, critically, the \textbf{Meta Quest $\rightarrow$ PICO 4 conversion} performed for each subsystem.

\subsection{Conversion Workflow Overview}

The migration was carried out in incremental phases, each verified before the next:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Identify and remove Meta dependencies.} The Meta/Oculus SDK and all \texttt{OVR} components were removed. A residual \texttt{OVRInput} reference in the bundled IngameDebugConsole caused a \texttt{CS0246} compile error and was stripped out.
  \item \textbf{Install the vendor-neutral XR stack.} XR Plug-in Management 4.4.0, XR Interaction Toolkit 2.5.4, and Input System 1.7.0 were added, and the PICO Unity Integration SDK (\texttt{com.unity.xr.picoxr}) was registered as the active XR provider.
  \item \textbf{Rebuild the camera rig and locomotion} (Section~\ref{sec:nav}).
  \item \textbf{Migrate interaction and UI} to controller-ray input (Section~\ref{sec:interaction}).
  \item \textbf{Fix scene flow} --- a dead \texttt{RoomScene} reference and stale Oculus configuration were removed.
  \item \textbf{Configure the Android build} --- IL2CPP, ARM64, minimum SDK 29, anti-aliasing through Unity Quality settings, PICO platform settings, and the application name (Toxland~G6).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Navigation Control}
\label{sec:nav}

The Meta \texttt{OVRCameraRig} was replaced with a Unity \textbf{XR Origin} rig, and the Meta \texttt{OVRInput} bindings were replaced with action-based input from the Input System (the XRI default input actions), with the PICO controller pose actions bound so that head and hand tracking drive the rig. Players move along the board using three complementary mechanisms:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Continuous joystick locomotion} via the XRI \texttt{DynamicMoveProvider}, driven by the controller thumbstick.
  \item \textbf{Teleportation} to board spaces using XRI Teleport Areas / Teleport Anchors.
  \item \textbf{Dice-roll movement} along the discrete board path.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interaction System}
\label{sec:interaction}

VR controller selection is provided by the XRI \textbf{XR Ray Interactor}: a pointer ray that the player aims at board spaces, household objects, and UI buttons. During the conversion the ray was made clearly visible to children by widening its line (from 0.005 to 0.02) and setting a cyan valid-target colour. \textbf{XR Grab Interactables} allow objects (e.g.\ the dice) to be picked up. For the educational UI --- the \emph{Betul/Salah} quiz panels --- an XRI UI input module with a tracked-device graphic raycaster and an EventSystem was added so that the controller ray can click world-space TextMeshPro buttons; this replaces the Meta interaction framework's UI handling.

\subsection{Scoring and Timing System}

Scoring is implemented as a lightweight persistent manager. \texttt{ScoreManager} holds a static score value and writes it to a TextMeshPro display every frame:

\begin{lstlisting}[language={[Sharp]C}]
public class ScoreManager : MonoBehaviour
{
    public static int scoreValue = 0;
    public TextMeshProUGUI score;

    void Update()
    {
        score.text = "Score: " + scoreValue;
    }
}
\end{lstlisting}

Quiz answers adjust the score through \texttt{BetulAndSalah} ($+2$ for a correct/``Right'' answer, $-2$ for ``Wrong''), after which the answer buttons are hidden, a correct/incorrect indicator is shown, and an explanation panel appears after a short delay:

\begin{lstlisting}[language={[Sharp]C}]
public void clickBtn()
{
    btnName.SetActive(false);
    correctWrongName.SetActive(true);
    if (correctWrongName.name == "Right")  ScoreManager.scoreValue += 2;
    else if (correctWrongName.name == "Wrong") ScoreManager.scoreValue -= 2;
    Invoke("DisplayExplanation", 3f); // show explanation after 3s
}
\end{lstlisting}

Ladder bonus moves are delivered through \texttt{BtnOKManager}, which invokes a configurable \texttt{ExtraMoveEvent} when the player confirms a correct Good-Practice action. The current build uses a \textbf{score-based} progression rather than a countdown timer; points accrue from correct safety decisions, quiz answers, hazard identification, and game completion.

\subsection{Sound and Feedback}

Feedback is delivered through three channels: (i)~\textbf{audio} --- a looping background-music track (\texttt{background-music.mp3}) sets a light-hearted tone, and a ``Button Pop'' click confirms each UI selection; (ii)~\textbf{visual} --- a Right/Wrong indicator image appears immediately after each quiz answer, followed by a written explanation panel; and (iii)~\textbf{kinaesthetic / spatial} --- ladder and trap squares physically move the player forward or backward along the board.

\subsection{Welcome Screen and End Screen}

The application retains a dedicated welcome screen introducing the game on launch. It is driven by \texttt{WelcomeController}, which shows the panel on launch, optionally pauses game time (\texttt{Time.timeScale = 0}) while the panel is visible without affecting head tracking or the controller ray, and resumes play when the Start button is clicked:

\begin{lstlisting}[language={[Sharp]C}]
void Awake()
{
    gameObject.SetActive(true);
    if (pauseWhileVisible) Time.timeScale = 0f;
}
public void OnStartClicked()
{
    if (pauseWhileVisible) Time.timeScale = 1f;
    gameObject.SetActive(false);
}
\end{lstlisting}

On reaching the final space, the end screen displays the player's final score. (The online Photon login flow was removed from the build --- the build's scene list contains only \texttt{MainScene} --- so the application boots directly to the welcome screen; the Photon SDK and login scene remain in the project but are unused in this single-player build.)

\subsection{User Manual}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Install} the \texttt{Toxland.apk} onto the PICO 4 (via SideQuter/ADB \texttt{adb install Toxland.apk}, or the PICO Developer tools).
  \item \textbf{Launch} ``Toxland G6'' from the PICO library; put on the headset and grip both controllers.
  \item On the \textbf{Welcome Screen}, aim the controller ray at the \textbf{Start} button and pull the trigger.
  \item \textbf{Move} with the thumbstick (continuous walking) or point the ray at a board space and press teleport. Use the dice to advance along the board.
  \item At each space, \textbf{read the statement} and answer \emph{Betul} (true) or \emph{Salah} (false) with the controller ray. Watch the Right/Wrong feedback and explanation.
  \item Each \textbf{correct answer} adds~2 points; each wrong answer subtracts~2.
  \item \textbf{Reach the final space} to finish; your final score is shown on the end screen.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

\subsection{Challenges and Solutions}

The conversion from Meta Quest to PICO 4 surfaced several concrete challenges:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Compile-breaking SDK references.} Removing the Oculus SDK left an \texttt{OVRInput} reference in the bundled IngameDebugConsole, producing a \texttt{CS0246} error. \emph{Solution:} the Meta-specific input call was removed so the project compiled cleanly against the new XR stack.
  \item \textbf{Editor and toolkit incompatibility.} XR Interaction Toolkit 2.5.4 requires a newer editor than the original Unity~2019.4.17f1. \emph{Solution:} the project was upgraded to Unity~2022.3.62f3 LTS before installing the new packages.
  \item \textbf{Camera rig and input remapping.} The Meta \texttt{OVRCameraRig} and \texttt{OVRInput} bindings have no direct PICO equivalent. \emph{Solution:} they were rebuilt as an XR Origin rig with action-based Input System bindings and explicitly bound PICO controller pose actions.
  \item \textbf{Controller-driven UI.} The Meta interaction framework previously handled UI; the world-space quiz panels did not respond to the controller ray after migration. \emph{Solution:} an XRI UI input module, tracked-device graphic raycaster, and EventSystem were added so the ray can click TextMeshPro buttons.
  \item \textbf{Ray visibility for children.} The default interactor ray was too thin to be obvious to young users. \emph{Solution:} the line width was increased and a cyan valid-target colour applied.
  \item \textbf{Scene and build configuration.} A dead \texttt{RoomScene} reference and stale Oculus settings broke scene flow, and the Android build needed PICO-appropriate settings. \emph{Solution:} the dead reference was removed and the build configured for IL2CPP/ARM64, minimum SDK~29, mobile-appropriate Unity Quality (anti-aliasing) settings, and PICO platform settings; a test build succeeded and the APK was verified running on a physical PICO~4 (see the recorded presentation).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future Improvements}

Future work includes: adding voiced narration so pre-literate children can play without reading; expanding the five rooms with more scenarios and hazard variety; introducing adaptive difficulty as in~\cite{roadsafety2021}; adding localisation (e.g.\ Bahasa Melayu / Mandarin); strengthening comfort options (vignetting, snap-turn) to reduce motion sickness in young users; and a formal learning-effectiveness study with a pre/post-test design.
